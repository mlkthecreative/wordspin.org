“Love not sleep lest thou come unto poverty”
Proverbs 20:13  ·  26th April, 2026  ·  MK.
“Love not sleep lest thou come unto poverty: Open thine eyes, and thou shall be satisfied with bread.”
I take this as literal and symbolic. We were made to work for our upkeep. In Literature classes (my favourite! How shocking!?) we were taught about outer meanings and inner meanings. Outer meaning here could be: if you love to sleep, then you will not eat, and you will fall into a desperate situation characterised by LACK. While this is true, it has bred a dangerous culture that frowns upon rest by linking it to laziness. This could be the reason why children in boarding schools (in parts of Africa) are not given the time to close their eyes and rest — because it is seen as a waste of time that could be used to do classwork or some sort of ‘show you are busy and focused’ activity. This ends up nurturing children who carry this mentality to their homes and children when they finally have them, causing a guilt or shame to develop in them for taking even a few minutes to themselves. Might I remind you that even God took a day to rest and appreciate his good creation in Genesis — in fact, after every day “he saw it was good.” By extension, he gave us a rest day. It is good to rest and it is good to appreciate the work that you have done. “PRACTICE GRATITUDE,” as therapists would say! Inner meaning here could be that we sleep on the things that matter and could feed us — give us bread. To me, this looks like not utilising the gifts and talents that have been given to us. How this links to ‘outer meaning’ is we work ourselves in a very linear way, or rather are made to move on a linear path that causes us NOT to see the benefit of using what comes so naturally to us (it is not entirely our fault!). I think this is part of the reason why we are encouraged to seek the Kingdom of God first, and then all these things shall be added to us. Why? Only he can PROPERLY help us / make us maintain the integrity of his design of us — from its goodness to its exponential growth potential being actualised: be fruitful and multiply. “Open thine eyes, and thou shall be satisfied with bread.”

It could be Heaven or it could be Hell.
I chose Heaven and so Heaven chose me.
I woke up with a strong sense of purpose and an even stronger sense of sleep. As has become customary that one messenger I cannot ignore woke me up- a mosquito! It was a good time for me to put out what I had washed the night before to dry in hopes that they would be dry by the time I was ready to pack my bag for an impromptu trip.
I did some WordSpin dev work for about an hour after a "failed" bible study attempt, had a bowl of porridge and went back to sleep. The weather was good! Even though it had been forecast to be rainy, it was bright and hot. My things dried- YES! "A prayer answered," I thought, "Thank you Lord!" I said.
Ironing took more time than I anticipated. Such delicate material were those deras! Easily burnt, easily creased... perfect for the beach like fine linen. Looking at the time though, I had no time to be spending even more than I had spent trying to straighten every single crease. I might be a little bit OCD- blame it on the genes, blame it on the way I was raised!
Fast forward and I ranged between short waves of anxiety and calm. Overall, I had "a peace" about the day that kept me grounded- faithful. I had a sense that the day would be good and that everything would work out just fine.
OBEDIENT LIL' MISSY
Do you believe that before something very good happens, something terrible happens? It's almost as if these odds have to balance themselves out.
I was encouraged to leave at 12:30 p.m, but planned to leave at 1 p.m. Somewhere between 1 and ten past one, I ordered an uber boda (motorbike). The rider was less than a minute away, which was unusual considering where I live. In fact(!) he was at the bottom of my road, sort of like he'd been waiting there (from a deeper point of view, it felt like he was meant to be there). I knew that with Nairobi traffic and where I needed to go, a bike would be the better option for me since I didn't have much to carry.
I noticed his number plate (777H) immediately and smiled, "aligned" I thought. When we got to a stretch that I'd usually use with no problem for years (with the day before being the most recent), we were stopped and told there was no way through. The man who stopped us demanded that the man turn off his engine and that I get off. S@#&!
Obedient miss me hopped off. In the voice of Trey Songz, "time is not on our side". I thought that if it took more than five minutes to negotiate, I'd have to get someone new. Next thing I knew, the rider bolted away and I was left standing there, potentially stranded and bagless (because he had my main bag). S@#&!!
A normal person would get very emotional about this. I felt a wave of worry and anxiety come over me but worked to calm it down. How? Well, I knew panic and anger would waste my energy. Negativity eats at the soul - FEAR and ANGER eat at the soul in a way nothing else can - we lose more than we ever gain by giving them space to grow. So, I chose faith. Faith said that I'd meet him on the other side- that maybe he'd just driven to the nearest matatu (bus) stage and I would find him there. So, I walked. IT WAS HOT!
As I walked I thought about how the worst thing that could happen was not retrieving my bag, but at least I had all I needed to get on the train Mombasa. Though it would be at an extra cost, I could figure out my clothing situation at my destination. I also did what my grandfather calls a phone call to Heaven. It is an emergency dial to God, asking him for whatever you need. I asked that he put it in that man's heart to in the least not rob me, and in the best case scenario come back and take me so that I didn't miss my train. Ordering a new ride didn't look like a good idea considering the potential wait time. After my request, I tried to reach the rider two times as I neared the stage. No success, particularly because my phone was acting up. "I'll just try when I am right at the stage," I thought. Just like that, an incoming call from him came in. He told me that I could get my bag from the Total Petrol Station.
Now, I don't know whether it was my brain that had been fried by the heat or the waves of emotion that had temporarily wiped my memory (or both) but I could not visualise where he was telling me to go to find my bag. It's possible I was tired too, considering I hadn't really slept the night before. This all just sounds like a recipe for disaster, doesn't it?!
Despite that, I still kept my cool on the call. Why? It could have been worse! He could've decided to use what was in my bag as payment, FAR MORE than what he'd make from the overall trip. Instead, he called telling me where he wanted to leave it for me to get. He also didn't sound like he expected me to pay him, although, I didn't mind tipping him for his north facing moral code.
I told him exactly where I was and he asked "how?!" and I said, "I walked. I thought that I would find you at the stage and we would continue with the trip".
"Wah. Umejaribu Mama... should I.... can I come back for you?" he asked.
I told him yes and that he'd find me right at the stage.
"Don't worry. I am coming. Don't worry," he said.
MEET ME HALFWAY
On the ride, I wondered (awed) about what had just happened. How everything seemed to be going wrong, but how it went it right because I believed it could and would. Sure, even before that, there was a sense (call it a promise) that the day would be good.
I didn't IGNORE what was wrong, I just chose to focus on what was right, and what could make it even 'righter'. Wisdom says if not for God then for yourself- learn to preserve your energy! These are the words that I remembered though: If you have faith as a mustard seed, you will say to this mountain, "Move from here to there," and it will move; and nothing will be impossible for you.
I sang songs of worship all the way to the station, with a short battery change stop in between. At the charging station, I told him that I thought he had abandoned me and he said, "No. I can't". Later, at the train station, the rider and I had a short exchange about men like the one who stopped us, and what he had been through because of them: losing much needed money to posers. He explained that if there was a chance to escape he would (which he did)! I understood, we talked for a bit more and he blessed my trip. I gave what I promised in my heart and moved on.
Check in was chaotic given the time. "I guess everyone goes for the 3 p.m train!" I thought. It was hot, my two bags were a strain to carry and my body was tense despite my certainty in spirit. I had the patience of a monk some might say!
People were very nice to me at the station. Nice in a way that felt out of the ordinary, including an armed security officer asking if he could help me. In my experience, they usually keep off unless it's a matter of security. Now, I consider that it may just be that I looked more tired than I am leading on, LOL!
Sleep deprivation/lack of proper rest, having to quiet a storm that wanted to rise in me- stress control- walking in the heat to a destination I (if I'm being honest) was not "logically" guaranteed to find a need, only just hoping and praying. Yeah, I was QUITE TIRED, but not the kind of tire one is to regret. The reward supercedes the output, always.
My plan to be on the boarding path at 2:45 p.m was accurate. You would think that time went back by a bit to make sure this happened as I had imagined it. "A peace" I say!
On the train, I was sat next to a refreshing character who kept me chatting through the five hour, thirty minute ride. He was not only helpful, but quite generous. His energy was free of all the things that could hurt me and that was comforting considering how the world can be: Draining. But then again, it could be Heaven or it could be Hell, I chose Heaven, so Heaven chose me.
What I had planned for the train ride was WordSpin, writing and sleep. What I got? Inspiration, connection and food (and so here I am with a lil' chicken nugget for you to chew on, lol!).
The moral of the story is BELIEVE IN GO(O)D THINGS and go(o)d things will come to you no matter what your situation is. Since I believe in one who created all good things, my faith was in Him and what He said. It always starts with a promise then we have to meet Him halfway by faith and obedience.
It's always easier when you're not the one carrying everything on your own. When you know there is a higher power, one who is not vindictive- looking for mistakes, but loving - looking out for you, then life becomes more abundant because you draw water from a well that never runs out. You never have to worry or overthink, you just have to hand it over and the rest will be done for you. Even those wounds and scars that you have, they become marks of beauty because of what you gain from them. Yes, even those painful lessons are valuable, especially because knowing the other side, the badness it can bring, makes you appreciate the side that takes and keeps the badness away. It can either affirm your faith or your fear - it's all a matter of perspective and I highly recommend FAITH!
PS My "failed" Bible study became a lived practical on how living the faith works. So, it was not a failed Bible study at all!

When I was twelve years old my grandfather signed me up for bible camp. I was very upset with him because it was a teens camp and I was not a teen. Furthermore, I didn't want to be lonely and I thought I would be because of it. I didn't want more isolation.
I was the baby of the camp and that came with perks. Even though it came with perks, I didn't like being "awwwed" at all the time because it made me feel awkward and unappreciated for the depth I carried.
It so happened that two girls who knew me from school were at the camp. They were from the highschool section of my boarding school. They spoke highly of me and my knowledge on phones and music. They thought I was quite mature and liked hearing my take on things. I felt respected by them.
During a morning group discussion/fellowship a question was asked, "What do you think about?". Everyone in my group expressed some form of ambition and anxiety attached to it: future — exams, success, university, career path, marriage, success. I wondered how hard those determiner exams were considering the way they spoke. I also thought about how far my mind was from them and considered lying to fall in line.
When it got to my turn, on came the awwws, googly eyes and "do you think about cartoons and playing?" Haha, that was cute and I wished!
"I think about death".
I didn't lie, I couldn't lie! There was a loud silence in their pause and then a question about whether it was suicidal. I said no and then explained.
What if the sky is glass? What if it breaks one day and it rains glass? How do we hide? Where do we hide? Some people will have shade while others will not, but eventually the broken glass will hurt them because they will have to walk on it. What about the people in the open? What a bloody and painful death that would be. What if the ground split open and I fell in a crack, no matter how much I tried to avoid the cracks. Or what if my legs were on either side of splitting earth and it split me in half? What is beyond death? Is it just darkness? Blankness? Is there really a "better place"?
They were speechless.
These are the things I'd think about at twelve. I'd visualise a yellow chick doing its best to survive in a shower of glass shards and open earth — somehow able to miss imminent death. But where was this chick running to? To who even? Why was it even running considering that there was nothing to live for (yet?) Where was it safe? Why was its will to survive so strong? There was nobody to save her but she wished she could save others she'd watch get cut and swallowed.
I was then asked why. Why were my thoughts so dark and deep? They were interesting (even entertaining) to listen to but VERY CONCERNING. As I write this I see the story-worthiness in this and see why they were hooked.
I then talked about how I didn't know what I would do if my grandparents died. They were old. Where would I go? What would I do? Who would I have? I depended on them. Things were complicated for me in ways I was not willing to get into (or even "allowed to"), but I did express that my grandmother had cancer. I wished it would go away, but it wasn't that simple. I wished I could save her but it just didn't work like that. I didn't like her low and slow days, I didn't like the ones where she was in pain — I wished I could take it all away. The same went for my mother who was not well in an incurable way.
What I see now is I was worried and anxious about the things that were not in my control, especially as they related to disease and death, to loved ones and losing them, to what I would have left if they left which was inevitable. I also questioned my drive because somehow, something kept me going. I recounted the story of my birth as it was told to me, how I beat all odds and continued to. The reason? I did not know. But, death and I were tied in an extraordinary way, and now that I look at it, the real relationship might be with LIFE. The duality of nature: there can be no good without bad, no life without death, no sweet without sour, no salty without bland, URGH!
I was still a baby, I knew that, and that's probably why I'd always see a chick running. It is like how when a person is anxious they dream about being chased by something. Mine were not dreams but thoughts — "imaginations" I had with my eyes wide open, looking at the world around me.
These gory images that I saw reflected how much it affected me. I also expressed how I didn't like how people didn't seem to take me seriously, and how I was often dismissed for having a wild imagination. I admitted I wanted to lie when my turn came but because I would be there for a few days, I thought why not?! "I won't have to see them again so they can get every single detail of what runs through my mind..." I may have even admitted that I tried to end it all for myself but to no success. I had to accept that there was no dying for me (which is a wild thing to say). Not in such a way and not soon. It was as if life wanted me alive.
That ended up getting me a lot of attention — I MEAN A LOT. Some wanted to comfort me while most wanted to hear more from me because they liked what I had to say. Today, I can confidently say they were attracted to my dark philosophy and wanted to hear more about it. It was strange, but I just rolled with it because it felt good to be accepted and listened to. I became the old woman in a young body that they would sit around to hear from for hours.
Back then, I was far more talkative than I am now. I wasn't as caring of the things I would say — I didn't see the point of lying though I could hide things in metaphors and be honest about raw details being too personal to get into. I eventually grew into filtering myself so much that writing became that one place where it could all come out without obstruction. I am now better expressed in written word than I am in spoken word, unless it is previously written for me to have a safe path to follow. In some way, WordSpin is my solace and I am just now realising that!
I was taught to worry about the child my words were not appropriate for, or the adult my words would be too much for — causing an uncomfortable feeling in them and judgement on the ones raising me. It was almost always inappropriate.
Now, I am unhappy that when I speak the filtering happens in real time. I struggle to get my points across as coherently as say twelve year old me would verbally. Writing ended up taking over for verbal loss because it was the only way I could express these things without hurdles and walls.
I was surprised the other day reading through my old diaries from that timeline how my coping mechanism had a coping mechanism. If I wasn't as self aware, even in the throws of it, I would be very split now. If there is something a reader should pick from this, it is that children should not be ignored or even forced into uniformity. Their uniqueness should be embraced lest they go through whatever crisis I had to go through for years that I am just now working (though not alone) to emerge from.
Don't get me wrong, I can speak. I am just far too calculated that it exhausts me. So, I'd rather not try! I wonder what the process is for someone like me wanting to reverse this — not looking to lose the writing skill, but nurture the neglected tongue that was made to speak from an abundant heart and mind.

Mama Was Right
27th May, 2026  ·  MK.
Are umber and henna the same thing?
Wait, no! They cannot be because a henna lady told me it comes from a plant. Umber is described as coming from the earth. One might argue that plants come from the earth and get off on a technicality, HA!
Umber makes me think of henna. There are no wrong answers in this game, just memories and stories mapped through word triggers. This triggers the memory of a professional henna artist who grew to like me enough to give me what I wanted for free because I loved it so much.
The thing about it was that come Nairobi time, I'd be scrubbing my skin until it was visibly pink-red, lighter than the rest of me and swollen because it showing in school could get me in big trouble. For context, I am a dark skinned woman, to get to this extent meant I did the most! This may be why my aunt didn't like paying for it and eventually refused altogether — she must have known it wasn't worth it because of what I'd do to myself to get it off when school was opening and the henna was still quite fresh looking. If not that, it would generally be a waste of money because I wouldn't even enjoy it for long.
But WOW! Can you imagine what I had to do to get henna off my skin in a matter of days?! Henna?!!!
I learned to start the process early after the first few times. The pain taught me to do things in intervals rather than at one go. This way, I wouldn't literally be forcing off parts of my skin to go with the pigment, and nursing it with vaseline that would melt because of the heat emanating from the scrubbed and extra sensitive area, jumping at every touch.
It's funny writing this because it turns out that I'd never learn! On one end I wanted to always see what was on me while on the other that meant the world could see it too. What mattered more though, was that I got the pretty art when I could because where in Nairobi could I sit by the beach while I got drawn on? NOWHERE!
Going back a few years, there was a tattoo parlour not so far from where we lived. I begged what feels like almost every day to be taken there. I didn't know what I wanted, but I wanted A TATTOO! We lived in Maryland at the time so to find a sleeved person was not rare compared to Kenya where this was not a thing, and still isn't. I liked the patterns and the colours, all I saw was art and I'm an artsy babe — that much has not changed! I was a six year old obsessed with tattoos and I didn't want to hear the excuses!
My mother asked me if I had ever seen a child with a tattoo and I said no but I could be the first! Don't limit the opportunity now! She asked me what would happen if I regretted what I got? Tattoos were permanent. Obviously, I didn't care. The henna story should tell you exactly how I would have behaved come regret, LOL! I'm not even going to give you the satisfaction of admitting that she was right! 👀
"It's illegal to let you do it, you're a child, you have to be at least 16 but here the age limit is 18." She also told me that my skin and their skin was different so it probably wouldn't look as nice and I wouldn't want it anymore. Blah Blah Blah like that Ke$ha song. They were all excuses to me therefore I remained insistent. So insistent that I imagine they avoided the tattoo parlour route whenever we were together because I would start demanding and begging to be taken in for one.
There was a boy in my class who I liked. He got fire on his arm 🔥. No, it was not a real tattoo, it was one of those stick-ons and I had never heard of that! It matched the gym teacher's tattoo (who I also liked). He got a pat on his back for it from him and boy was I jealous — attention starved and fueled! Again, my parents heard from me. Crush fuel is even more intense because it has to do with wanting validation from the ones your eyes are starry for. On one end my tattoo love was affirmed and on another, I had an in to be noticed for something other than being black and from Africa.
One day, when in view of the parlour, I started. My mum said, "Fine. Let's see what they say about how old you need to be to get one..." This came after I had thrown quite the tantrum on the street. I'm starting to think I may have been a little bit bratty! Inside, there were lovely examples of what I could get on the wall and a woman at a desk. I thought, "this is it."
"Can you please tell her how old she needs to be to get a tattoo?"
"18," the lady said, "How old are you sweetie?"
"6" I said.
"Aw, yeah, we can't do that. But when you're 18, you can come and get one."
"But that's too far away..." I said with a broken heart. I can't remember the rest of the interaction because I was in my sad sad feelings about it. I was probably crying. Later, I resolved to that being my 18th birthday present. 16th if I was in a state that allowed it!
Today, I have no interest in tattoos and one would never know it was THAT deep for me. It is probably for the best because I'd probably end up looking like Wiz Khalifa if I started early. I'd be a walking canvas and it probably wouldn't look as good as I imagined it would. I'd take my skin through it to correct my in the heat of the moment decisions. Yeah, my Mama was right!
