“Love not sleep lest thou come unto poverty”
Proverbs 20:13  ·  26th April, 2026  ·  MK.
Experience All Ages
“Love not sleep lest thou come unto poverty: Open thine eyes, and thou shall be satisfied with bread.”
I take this as literal and symbolic. We were made to work for our upkeep. In Literature classes (my favourite! How shocking!?) we were taught about outer meanings and inner meanings. Outer meaning here could be: if you love to sleep, then you will not eat, and you will fall into a desperate situation characterised by LACK. While this is true, it has bred a dangerous culture that frowns upon rest by linking it to laziness. This could be the reason why children in boarding schools (in parts of Africa) are not given the time to close their eyes and rest — because it is seen as a waste of time that could be used to do classwork or some sort of ‘show you are busy and focused’ activity. This ends up nurturing children who carry this mentality to their homes and children when they finally have them, causing a guilt or shame to develop in them for taking even a few minutes to themselves. Might I remind you that even God took a day to rest and appreciate his good creation in Genesis — in fact, after every day “he saw it was good.” By extension, he gave us a rest day. It is good to rest and it is good to appreciate the work that you have done. “PRACTICE GRATITUDE,” as therapists would say! Inner meaning here could be that we sleep on the things that matter and could feed us — give us bread. To me, this looks like not utilising the gifts and talents that have been given to us. How this links to ‘outer meaning’ is we work ourselves in a very linear way, or rather are made to move on a linear path that causes us NOT to see the benefit of using what comes so naturally to us (it is not entirely our fault!). I think this is part of the reason why we are encouraged to seek the Kingdom of God first, and then all these things shall be added to us. Why? Only he can PROPERLY help us / make us maintain the integrity of his design of us — from its goodness to its exponential growth potential being actualised: be fruitful and multiply. “Open thine eyes, and thou shall be satisfied with bread.”

But then again, it could be Heaven or it could be Hell, I chose Heaven, so Heaven chose me.
What I had planned for the train ride was WordSpin, writing and sleep. What I got? Inspiration, connection and food (and so here I am with a lil' chicken nugget for you to chew on, lol!).
The moral of the story is BELIEVE IN GO(O)D THINGS and go(o)d things will come to you no matter what your situation is. Since I believe in one who created all good things, my faith was in Him and what He said. It always starts with a promise then we have to meet Him halfway by faith and obedience.
It's always easier when you're not the one carrying everything on your own. When you know there is a higher power, one who is not vindictive- looking for mistakes, but loving - looking out for you, then life becomes more abundant because you draw water from a well that never runs out. You never have to worry or overthink, you just have to hand it over and the rest will be done for you. Even those wounds and scars that you have, they become marks of beauty because of what you gain from them. Yes, even those painful lessons are valuable, especially because knowing the other side, the badness it can bring, makes you appreciate the side that takes and keeps the badness away. It can either affirm your faith or your fear - it's all a matter of perspective and I highly recommend FAITH!

When I was twelve years old my grandfather signed me up for bible camp. I was very upset with him because it was a teens camp and I was not a teen. Furthermore, I didn't want to be lonely and I thought I would be because of it. I didn't want more isolation.
I was the baby of the camp and that came with perks. Even though it came with perks, I didn't like being "awwwed" at all the time because it made me feel awkward and unappreciated for the depth I carried.
It so happened that two girls who knew me from school were at the camp. They were from the highschool section of my boarding school. They spoke highly of me and my knowledge on phones and music. They thought I was quite mature and liked hearing my take on things. I felt respected by them.
During a morning group discussion/fellowship a question was asked, "What do you think about?". Everyone in my group expressed some form of ambition and anxiety attached to it: future — exams, success, university, career path, marriage, success. I wondered how hard those determiner exams were considering the way they spoke. I also thought about how far my mind was from them and considered lying to fall in line.
When it got to my turn, on came the awwws, googly eyes and "do you think about cartoons and playing?" Haha, that was cute and I wished!
"I think about death".
I didn't lie, I couldn't lie! There was a loud silence in their pause and then a question about whether it was suicidal. I said no and then explained.
What if the sky is glass? What if it breaks one day and it rains glass? How do we hide? Where do we hide? Some people will have shade while others will not, but eventually the broken glass will hurt them because they will have to walk on it. What about the people in the open? What a bloody and painful death that would be. What if the ground split open and I fell in a crack, no matter how much I tried to avoid the cracks. Or what if my legs were on either side of splitting earth and it split me in half? What is beyond death? Is it just darkness? Blankness? Is there really a "better place"?
They were speechless.
These are the things I'd think about at twelve. I'd visualise a yellow chick doing its best to survive in a shower of glass shards and open earth — somehow able to miss imminent death. But where was this chick running to? To who even? Why was it even running considering that there was nothing to live for (yet?) Where was it safe? Why was its will to survive so strong? There was nobody to save her but she wished she could save others she'd watch get cut and swallowed.
I was then asked why. Why were my thoughts so dark and deep? They were interesting (even entertaining) to listen to but VERY CONCERNING. As I write this I see the story-worthiness in this and see why they were hooked.
I then talked about how I didn't know what I would do if my grandparents died. They were old. Where would I go? What would I do? Who would I have? I depended on them. Things were complicated for me in ways I was not willing to get into (or even "allowed to"), but I did express that my grandmother had cancer. I wished it would go away, but it wasn't that simple. I wished I could save her but it just didn't work like that. I didn't like her low and slow days, I didn't like the ones where she was in pain — I wished I could take it all away. The same went for my mother who was not well in an incurable way.
What I see now is I was worried and anxious about the things that were not in my control, especially as they related to disease and death, to loved ones and losing them, to what I would have left if they left which was inevitable. I also questioned my drive because somehow, something kept me going. I recounted the story of my birth as it was told to me, how I beat all odds and continued to. The reason? I did not know. But, death and I were tied in an extraordinary way, and now that I look at it, the real relationship might be with LIFE. The duality of nature: there can be no good without bad, no life without death, no sweet without sour, no salty without bland, URGH!
I was still a baby, I knew that, and that's probably why I'd always see a chick running. It is like how when a person is anxious they dream about being chased by something. Mine were not dreams but thoughts — "imaginations" I had with my eyes wide open, looking at the world around me.
These gory images that I saw reflected how much it affected me. I also expressed how I didn't like how people didn't seem to take me seriously, and how I was often dismissed for having a wild imagination. I admitted I wanted to lie when my turn came but because I would be there for a few days, I thought why not?! "I won't have to see them again so they can get every single detail of what runs through my mind..." I may have even admitted that I tried to end it all for myself but to no success. I had to accept that there was no dying for me (which is a wild thing to say). Not in such a way and not soon. It was as if life wanted me alive.

There was a man in the corner of my room every night. All he did was watch me. I did not have distinct features to work with, but he was shaped like a man — his silhouette was that of a man in a loose fitting suit and he always faced me meaning his eyes, though not visible, were on me.
I spent a few nights in my grandparents' room because I was afraid of the shadow man (this is what I called him). He is the reason why the light outside my bedroom was kept on and why my door had to be left open to let the light in. I was afraid of him.
There came a time my grandmother told me that I had to grow up. I had to come out of that "phase" I was in. She'd often tell the story of maize stalks turning into people cheering and a maize cob being a microphone in her mind when she'd fantasize about being a famous musician like Michael Jackson. That was all fantasy though, it was not real and so my shadow man could not be real.
I did not like that she did not believe me because I knew he was real and he did not give me good feelings inside. Trying to prove his existence was a futile endeavour because I was the only one who could see him, and he only came out at a certain time at night, and then disappeared at a certain time before the sun came up.
One night, I decided to give him the benefit of the doubt. Rather than an endless stare down until I hopefully fell asleep pretending I was not afraid (because I always thought showing fear gave him power), I spoke to it. I thought, "if nothing happens then Cûcû is right." I greeted him and nothing happened! I smiled — the kind of relief — and settled into safety, thinking about how Cûcû was right about him and how foolish I had been thinking that what I saw was real.
A phase. A phase. A phase. Children go through phases!
He widened his stance, crossing his arms as he did. I felt cold — TERROR! I told my Cûcû the following morning and she laughed about how "ahead" my mind was. What I had to say was entertaining to her at that point. She wanted more but I had no more to give except that his position had changed and it was even scarier for me.
Fast forward and I stole a rosary from my friend Dolly. The intention wasn't for the shadow man, I just thought they were cool but because I wasn't Catholic there was no point in buying me one so my requests were always rejected. Stealing was the next best thing since I had no money to buy one for myself. Yeah, I was a little bit of a thief back then and I think it made me a tad bit vulnerable to the shadow man.
On another night, I did more than call him to me, I gave him a presumed identity. Why? I wanted to give myself something to be comfortable about. Neither Cûcû nor Guka believed me and I was tired of being teased (mocked) about it. There and then, I imagined he was someone sent to look over me. A dead relative? A guardian angel? My father? I never knew my father but he might as well have been! I then thought, "NO!" Maybe it's been Guka all along! Maybe he watches over me as I sleep and that's why the two can never exist in the same place at the same time.
"Maybe it's a secret. Maybe this is how much they love me — how much Guka loves me."

"Guka is that you?" I asked.
"Guka I know it's you..." I said.
In the three instances nothing happened. Nothing about him changed. He didn't react or respond. He just stood there, arms crossed, legs wide, eyes fixed on me. I was disappointed, sad and afraid. I turned over to face the wall so I didn't have to face the direction in which he stood.
Three taps on my shoulder. I turned and voilà! There by my bed, standing over me, stood this shadow figure that still had no distinct features yet a body I could not see through. He had come to me. It's funny because I can't even say I was a believer like that yet deep down I knew it was the only way — my only defense.
